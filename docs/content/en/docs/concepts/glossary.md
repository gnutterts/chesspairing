### ARO (Average Rating of Opponents)

The arithmetic mean of the ratings of all opponents a player has faced in actual over-the-board games. Forfeited games are excluded. Used as a [tiebreaker](/docs/tiebreakers/).

### Berger table

A fixed rotation schedule used in [round-robin tournaments](/docs/concepts/round-robin/) to determine who plays whom in each round. The last player is held fixed while the remaining players rotate through positions. Defined in FIDE C.05 Annex 1.

### Blossom matching

An algorithm (Edmonds' maximum weight matching) that finds the optimal set of pairings in a graph where players are nodes and potential pairings are weighted edges. Used by the [Dutch](/docs/pairing-systems/dutch/) and [Burstein](/docs/pairing-systems/burstein/) pairers to produce globally optimal Swiss pairings. See [Blossom matching](/docs/algorithms/blossom/).

### Buchholz

A tiebreaker that sums the final scores of all opponents a player has faced. Multiple variants exist: full Buchholz, cut-1 (drop the lowest opponent score), cut-2 (drop the two lowest), median (drop highest and lowest), and median-2 (drop two highest and two lowest). See [tiebreakers](/docs/tiebreakers/).

### Bye

A round in which a player does not have an opponent. There are six types: PAB (pairing-allocated bye, worth 1 point by default), half-point bye (requested, worth 0.5), zero-point bye (requested, worth 0), absent (unexcused, 0 points), excused (notified in advance), and club commitment (absent for interclub duty). See [byes](/docs/concepts/byes/).

### Color preference

A player's computed preference for White or Black based on their game history. Comes in three strengths: **absolute** (color imbalance exceeds 1 game, or the player has had the same color two or more times in a row -- the preference must be granted), **strong** (imbalance of exactly 1, not absolute), and **mild** (no imbalance, but alternation from the last game is preferred). See [colors](/docs/concepts/colors/).

### Completability

A pre-matching technique used by the Dutch and Burstein systems to determine which player should receive the [bye](/docs/concepts/byes/) when there is an odd number of players. The algorithm tests whether removing a candidate still allows a complete matching of all remaining players. See [completability](/docs/algorithms/completability/).

### Downfloater

A player who is paired with an opponent from a lower [score group](/docs/concepts/floaters/). This happens when their own score group has an odd number of players or cannot be fully matched internally.

### Edge weight

A numeric value assigned to a potential pairing in the Blossom matching graph. Higher weights represent more desirable pairings. Edge weights encode all optimization criteria (color satisfaction, float history, score group distance) as a single multi-precision integer using a bit-field layout. See [Blossom matching](/docs/algorithms/blossom/).

### Exchange (Lim system)

A matching technique used in the [Lim pairing system](/docs/pairing-systems/lim/) where players within a score group are rearranged to find a valid set of pairings. Unlike transpositions which reorder only the lower half, exchanges can swap players between the two halves of the group.

### Float / Floater

When a player cannot be paired within their score group and must be matched against an opponent from a higher or lower group. See [floaters](/docs/concepts/floaters/).

### Forfeit

A game result where one or both players did not appear. Single forfeits (`1-0f` or `0-1f`) award points to the winner but exclude the game from pairing history and tiebreaker calculations. Double forfeits (`0-0f`) exclude the game from both scoring and pairing history entirely. See [forfeits](/docs/concepts/forfeits/).

### Game result

The outcome of a chess game. Seven values are recognized: White wins (`1-0`), Black wins (`0-1`), draw (`0.5-0.5`), pending (`*`), forfeit White wins (`1-0f`), forfeit Black wins (`0-1f`), and double forfeit (`0-0f`).

### Keizer system

A pairing and scoring system where players are ranked by an iteratively computed score that rewards playing against higher-ranked opponents. Pairing is top-down by rank: 1 vs 2, 3 vs 4, and so on, with repeat avoidance. See [Keizer](/docs/pairing-systems/keizer/) and [Keizer scoring](/docs/scoring/).

### Lexicographic pairing

A matching approach used by the [Double-Swiss](/docs/pairing-systems/double-swiss/) and [Team Swiss](/docs/pairing-systems/team/) systems. Instead of Blossom matching, pairings are generated by trying combinations in lexicographic (dictionary) order and evaluating them against quality criteria, accepting the first combination that satisfies all requirements.

### Pairing-Allocated Bye (PAB)

The bye given to one player when a tournament round has an odd number of active participants. Worth a full point by default. A player should not receive a PAB more than once per tournament. See [byes](/docs/concepts/byes/).

### Pairing number (TPN)

The Tournament Pairing Number assigned to each player based on their current score and initial ranking. TPN 1 is the highest-ranked active player. Recalculated each round. See [pairing numbers](/docs/concepts/pairing-numbers/).

### Score group

All active players with the same score (or pairing score, when acceleration is in effect) form a score group. Within a score group, players are ordered by TPN. Score groups are processed from highest to lowest during pairing.

### Scoring system

The method used to calculate player scores from game results. chesspairing implements three systems: [standard](/docs/scoring/) (1-0.5-0), [football](/docs/scoring/) (3-1-0), and [Keizer](/docs/scoring/) (iterative value-based scoring). Each system has configurable point values for wins, draws, losses, byes, forfeits, and absences.

### Sonneborn-Berger

A [tiebreaker](/docs/tiebreakers/) computed by summing, for each opponent, the opponent's final score multiplied by the player's result against that opponent (1 for a win, 0.5 for a draw, 0 for a loss). Rewards beating strong opponents and drawing with them over beating weak ones.

### Swiss system

A tournament format where players with similar scores are paired against each other in each round, without requiring every player to face every other player. Multiple Swiss variants exist, each with different rules for resolving pairing conflicts. See [Swiss system](/docs/concepts/swiss-system/).

### Tiebreaker

A secondary metric used to rank players who have the same score. chesspairing implements [25 tiebreakers](/docs/tiebreakers/) including Buchholz variants, Sonneborn-Berger, direct encounter, performance rating, and many others. Tiebreakers are applied in a configured order to resolve ties. See [tiebreaking](/docs/concepts/tiebreaking/).

### Tournament state

The complete snapshot of a tournament at a point in time, passed to pairing and scoring engines. Contains the player list, all completed rounds with game results and byes, the current round number, and configuration for the pairing and scoring systems. Engines operate on this read-only structure and never modify it directly.

### Transposition

A reordering of players within the lower half (S2) of a score group in the Dutch pairing system. Transpositions are tried systematically to find a valid set of pairings that satisfies the absolute criteria (no rematches, no absolute color conflicts). Distinguished from exchanges, which swap players between halves.

### TRF16

The FIDE Tournament Report File format, version 16. A text-based file format for exchanging tournament data. chesspairing can read, write, validate, and convert TRF16 files, and can convert between TRF documents and its internal `TournamentState` representation.

### Upfloater

A player who is paired with an opponent from a higher [score group](/docs/concepts/floaters/). The receiving group sends a downfloater; the player matched against the downfloater effectively floats up.

### Varma table

A lookup table used to assign federation-aware [pairing numbers](/docs/concepts/pairing-numbers/) in round-robin tournaments. Players from the same federation are distributed across four groups so they meet in later rounds rather than early ones. Supports up to 24 players. Defined in FIDE C.05 Annex 2. See [Varma tables](/docs/algorithms/varma-tables/).

### Virtual points (Baku acceleration)

Temporary bonus points added to a player's pairing score (not their real score) during the early rounds of a large Swiss tournament. Under Baku acceleration, top-ranked players (Group A) receive extra virtual points that place them in higher score groups, creating more varied first-round matchups. The bonus decreases over the accelerated rounds and eventually drops to zero. See [Swiss system](/docs/concepts/swiss-system/).
